# Claude Prompt Engineering

**Author:** Nik Bear Brown  
**Publisher:** Bear Brown LLC

---

## One-Sentence Pitch

Prompt engineering is not trick wording; it is the discipline of specifying work so Claude can do the right part and the human can judge the result.

## The Argument

This book argues that good Claude prompting is specification design. The point is not to coax the model with clever phrasing but to define the job, provide the right context, constrain the output, expose assumptions, require reasoning artifacts when useful, and build a review loop. A good prompt makes both the AI's task and the human's review easier.

Most readers arrive believing prompt engineering is about secret phrases or magic wording. This book replaces that model entirely. The reader leaves with a transferable discipline: task, context, constraints, examples, output format, evaluation criteria, and revision loop — applied to any domain, any audience, any recurring workflow.

The key insight the book defends is that a prompt is a work order, not a wish. Vague prompts do not fail because Claude is uncooperative; they fail because the work is underspecified. The human who learns to specify work clearly gains something more durable than a collection of winning prompts — they gain a method.

## The Gap

Prompt-tip lists give readers phrases to copy but no transferable principles. General prompt engineering books treat all models and contexts alike. Claude Code books focus on agentic coding pipelines. This book fills the gap in between: it teaches prompt engineering as a reusable specification discipline for Claude users who need reliable drafts, analysis, critique, research support, and revision workflows — without requiring programming knowledge or agentic tooling.

## The Reader

A student, educator, researcher, analyst, writer, or professional who uses Claude but gets inconsistent results. They know how to ask questions but do not yet know how to structure context, constraints, examples, and evaluation criteria so the work can be repeated and improved. They do not need to know how to code. They do need to supervise outputs. After finishing this book, they can design prompts that are inspectable, testable, and reusable — and they know which parts of the work still require their own judgment.

## High-Level Outline

**Part I — From Request to Specification (Foundations)**  
Chapters 0–6. Establishes the core discipline: the prompt as work order, the anatomy of a well-formed prompt, context hygiene, constraints, examples, evaluation criteria, and the revision loop. Replaces prompt tricks with a specification framework the reader can apply immediately.

**Part II — Applied Prompt Sets (Domain Workflows)**  
Chapters 7–11. Applies the specification discipline to real work: research and literature, writing and editing, data and analysis support, teaching and learning, and handoffs to Claude Code and Cowork. Each chapter builds a domain-specific prompt library grounded in the Act One framework.

**Part III — Synthesis and Reuse (Building a Prompt Practice)**  
Chapters 12–13. Teaches the reader to turn successful prompts into maintained workflow assets — prompt cards with purpose, inputs, constraints, output format, review criteria, and revision history. The capstone asks the reader to design, test, revise, and document a prompt workflow for a real recurring task.
